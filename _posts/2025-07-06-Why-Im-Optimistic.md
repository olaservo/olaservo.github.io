title: Why I'm Optimistic

Some of the biggest positive turning points in my life and career happened during the hardest times.  Work isn't life, but it provides part of the foundation, and I prefer my work-life balance to be a little blurry anyway.

<!--more-->

# 2001

I finished up high school amid large scale fear and grieving.  I found a lot of new friends through following the anti-fear and making embarrassing zines and protesting and going to shows.  This form of expression was exciting and energizing and annoyed a lot of people, including my parents.  Luckily I didn't get into drugs or anything really damaging, like they were absolutely sure I was doing. (I did unfortunately start smoking cigarettes, but that was just what you did at shows.)

# 2008

"Los Angeles" by Flying Lotus had just come out and the economy was in shambles.  I listened to the album over and over again on a tiny generic mp3 player.  A very generous graphic designer at the Professional Bowlers Association gave me an opportunity to be a developer intern there, which later turned into a full-time job.  Money-wise things weren't great, and we lived in a small white cement block bunker in a trailer park by the Sammamish River slough where I never had enough of my own personal space.  But this all led to my first full time employment beyond 'please let me create you a basically free website that you can then ask me to rearrange 2 pixels at a time.'  It gave me a sense of validation.
# 2020

So many things had to stop happening because of Covid, but some of these things were a blessing in disguise.  I found a lot of room to grow.  I thought I hated running but it turns out I don't, which I discovered through the gateway drug of a virtual half marathon, all the way into multi-hour trail runs where all I had to think about was nature, hydration, and eating snacks. At work, I got to be a part of bigger projects and get a wider point of view of Nordstrom as a whole.  Computer screens also equalize us physically to some extent.  Lets just say, I'm not a tall person and I tend to hide away from bright and noisy places.  Now I feel a lot less anxiety, even when I come back to the bright and noisy places, and I've heard the same from other people too.
# 202?

One of my memento mori-style reminders is a date: 4/13/2029.  I heard that an asteroid will come especially close to hitting the earth on that day.  I don't really think that an asteroid is likely to destroy the planet anytime soon, but I like to use this a vivid reminder of the temporary nature of 'normal.'  What I think is much more likely to happen is slower and already happening, like William Gibson's Jackpot - a gradual cascade that adds up to transformation rather than one big apocalyptic moment. A lot of the same lessons from the other big terrible milestones still apply.

For me, the key resource to stockpile against the next big change is curiosity.  I never felt I could rely on the pathways and maps that other people provided, vs. trusting in the people that helped me draw my own.  So I feel that following my curiosity and actively working against fear has been the only way to know the way.  There's been a lot of luck and some amount of safety nets along the way, like being able to get financial aid for my never-ending part-time education, and of course the people who gave me their time and attention when I needed it most.

Maybe I will finally grow up to be a volunteer park ranger, or maybe we will finally finish the album we've been working on since the previous 'before times' (2019). In the wake of whatever happens next, what I feel like I want most is more connection, both to myself and to others.
